One of the more fascinating aspects of working on The Internet Review reboot project was coming to grips with just how many damn times I had started over from scratch when it came to writing about technology online for the past ~30 years.

I literally had to draw diagrams and save many, many bookmarks from The Internet Archive's Wayback Machine just to keep track of all the different places my words had ended up (and subsequently forgotten) over the decades. When I first started publishing (really, it was blogging!) on the Web in 1996, I certainly had no idea how this medium would change and evolve over time‚Ä¶and myself along with it.

But if I could step into a real-life wayback machine and give my past self one simple piece of advice, it would be this:

Don't shut it down. Migrate it!

Instead of doing that, here's the pattern I kept repeating all throughout the late 90s into the 2000s and even the 2010s: I'd launch a new website or online project of some kind, flounder along for a while, get bored, and then shut it down. Fast forward six months‚Ä¶a year‚Ä¶two years, and I'd decide to give something sort-of similar a try‚Äîbut rather than parlay whatever success I might have had to begin with into this new venture, I'd essentially be starting over from scratch.

Content creators of the world: hear me! This is NOT how to go about things! üòÖ

While I think it's perfectly alright to launch new ventures rather than endlessly tweak just one, or be willing to step away from an idea that just isn't coming together, you've got to be smart about it. Find a way to keep a through line, make it easy for your audience to travel along with you as you experiment‚Ä¶take the time to craft an ongoing narrative of your artistic journey so you have a meaningful sense of legacy.

The modern incarnation of The Internet Review I worked on really helped me to see this, as its almost 30-year-old archive was pulled from a number of online properties I'd started (and mostly stopped) over the years‚Ä¶yet once all that content was gathered together in one place, I could finally get a clear picture of how I'd grown as an author and how I'd remained true to the same values in many cases over many years. Reviewing these materials, one minute I would laugh at something said decades ago which proved to be wildly inaccurate or just plain bad‚Ä¶and the next minute I would be amazed at a sentence I'd written in a past era which sounds exactly like something I'd say today.

That's the power of legacy, of having a sense of permanence and genuine personal history. I believe as a content creator working in such a transient medium as the Web, it's vitally important to put some extra effort into finding ways to maintain such permanence.

All the way back in 2012, having been inspired by the movie The Vow, I wrote about the importance of crafting your creative environment. In that case, I was talking about decorating a physical space with artifacts of your creativity and things which inspire your art‚Äîa tangible way to remind you who you are. While that's a slightly different concept than what I'm writing about here, it's all part and parcel of the same basic premise: building something which truly matters over the long term‚Äîeven if you're "merely" a content creator.

These days I'm measuring projects in years or decades, not months‚Äîeven if they do morph and grow considerably and perhaps even get renamed at times. I want to focus on evolution, not reincarnation. And trust me, that's not always easy! There's a certain impulse at times to "burn it all down" and rage quit. Maybe come back at it one day in the future with an entirely fresh perspective. But I've learned you can effectively short-circuit that misguided process by adopting a much better one:

Bake an "ebb-and-flow mindset" into your creative process. Recognize there will be times when you will need to let your field lie fallow for a beat, and that's totally OK. Expected even! People (myself included!) are always apologizing on social media that they haven't blogged in a while when they finally just posted something. Guess what?

There's nothing wrong with that!

The idea that you aren't successful as a content creator unless you're posting a mile-a-minute every day, all the time, without fail, without ever taking a break, is a lie. That has more to do with the punishment of bad Internet algorithms than anything related to the noble pursuit of good art. In fact, I would argue that a constant stream-of-publishing approach actually works against real artistic merit. (But that's probably a whole essay unto itself.)

Dearest reader, I may not be Lady Whistledown, but I know enough to have embraced the truth that for everything there is a season. So rather than scramble to produce content like a maniac, burn out, feel guilty, and rage quit your projects‚Äîand then rinse-and-repeat twenty more times later in life‚Äîtry the better approach: evolution, not reincarnation.
