Over the lifetime of the internet, we've seen many waves of technologies come and go for allowing disparate throngs of people to coalesce around a particular topic for ongoing discussion. Arguably, this was the first "killer app" for the internet. File sharing was all well and good, but when you could actually converse with people around the world about your favorite movies or an esoteric program or a language or a philosophy or a game? Dynamite.

I'd like to look at some of these ebbs and flows of internet technologies, as we enter yet another era of the formation of interest groups called the "threadiverse" (threads + fediverse). More on that in a few moments.

Usenet / Mailing Lists

Predating even the World-Wide Web (we're talking 80s tech here!), both newsgroups (aka Usenet) and listservs (aka group mailing lists) allowed people to join in and participate in discussions on a near-infinite number of topics. Just find the newsgroup or listserv which tickled your fancy, and you were part of a potentially globe-spanning conversation. And don't forget about the flame wars‚Ä¶epic!

Indie Web Forums

Once the World-Wide Web really kicked into high gear, we witnessed the rise of independently-run forums on all sorts of interest-based websites. Remember phpBB? I myself ran forums on a number of occasions, all on topics ranging from technology to music to games. Forums sometimes accompanied blogs, and in certain cases even served as a blog commenting system. Some still do to this very day!

Centralized Destinations: Facebook / Digg / Reddit

As with so many stories of the evolution of the web in recent decades, starting in the early-to-mid 2000s we began to see consolidation and centralization around a small number of well-funded and oft-hyped properties. Google Groups and eventually Facebook Groups became an easy way to set up discussions revolving around particular themes. Sites like Digg and later Reddit served as "link aggregators" which could offer forum-like discussion mechanics around topics of interest. SEO "juice" ensured that the most popular sites would return the fastest Google search results, so the bigger you were, the bigger you'd remain. We were all sucked in. Escape‚Ä¶not so easy!

Chat Servers: Slack / Discord

Ephemeral chat rooms had long been a thing on the internet, with even protocols like IRC being in heavy rotation, but the emergence of Slack and later Discord proved you could blend concepts from email, chat, and discussion forums together to provide new ways of bringing folks together around particular subjects or tools or organizations. Even now, I personally am involved in a number of Discord servers and have made many nerd friends this way!

Going Back to the Future: Hello ‚ÄúThreadiverse‚Äù

Which brings us to an astounding moment in internet history. With the meltdown of Twitter in the age of Elon bringing with it a resurgence of interest in decentralized social networking protocols and the rapid growth of the ActivityPub-based "fediverse" (listen to my recent Just a Spec podcast episode all about it with my good friend Ayush), we have now begun to see a parallel explosion take place. The bigwigs at Reddit, seeing the dumpster fire that is today's Twitter and wanting a piece of that action (for reasons which are utterly beyond me‚Äîthough I predicted this MONTHS ago), have decided to screw the pooch and go to war with their moderator community and third-party app ecosystem.

Which has provided a welcome opportunity to do to Reddit what we've already been doing to Twitter: take a page from the web of old and set up a federated network that's of the people, by the people, and for the people.

The primary software projects (and ActivityPub-based instances thereof), Kbin and Lemmy, are on a rapid growth trajectory, adding tens of thousands of people (possibly hundreds of thousands of people) on a daily basis! And what's so exciting about this new opportunity is not only can you create a new community on, say, kbin.social, just like you might create a new subreddit‚Äîyou can simply set up and run your own "threadiverse" instance if you so wish! Like with Mastodon et al., people from one instance can upvote, converse, and otherwise engage with content from another instance. I have an account on kbin.social, but I can follow communities on other instances running Kbin, or Lemmy, or potentially any other compatible software! And because it's all ActivityPub under the hood, there's even a degree of federation with Mastodon and other microblogging-style services. I can post or comment in a threadiverse community, and then boost it from my main fediverse account. Everything interoperates. Whoa! If Not Now, When? If Not Us, Who?

I see nothing but HUGE opportunity and upside with the rise of the threadiverse. For content creators in particular, this could end up being an even bigger deal than the initial spike of the Twitter-like fediverse with Mastodon et al. Our biggest challenge is building a sense of community around the art and topics and causes we care about. Just posting on your own profile to your own followers can only get you so far. What if a community‚Äîor multiple communities‚Äîcould spring up around what you're creating or promoting? And what if you could actively participate in other communities and establish real trust such that, when you occasionally share what you're working on, it's not perceived as spam but rather as valuable content well worth sharing?

And unlike the Google Groups or Facebook Groups or subreddits or Slack servers or whatevers of the past era of the internet, this time we're setting things up right. No centralized services run by VC-backed bozos. Instead: open protocols. Powering open source software. You can pick an instance you like, running the software you like. Or you can run your own damn instance. It's entirely up to you! You're in control! You, the creator! Not Mark Zuckerberg. Not Elon Musk. (Though, let's admit, it'd be awesome to see them in a cage fight.) Not Steve Huffman. Not some venture capital firm in Silicon Valley looking to cash out. Not some smarmy board of investors.

Creators of the World: Unite!

The key missing ingredient to the so-called "creator economy" over the last decade or more has been actual ownership of the platforms we use to publish, share, sell, and discuss our work. The fediverse, and the threadiverse along with it, seeks to upend that unacceptable imbalance. We finally have the rare opportune moment to right past wrongs and build a better internet‚Ä¶one that puts creators, fan communities, and independently-run operators in charge. Carpe diem!

We might never get another chance like this one.
